A BABY ISSUE?

LOW BIRTH RATES IN TAIWAN

FUTURE

By Jamie Lin Pinzon

The CIA predicted Taiwan's total fertility rate (TFR) will be the lowest in the world in 2021.

Among the 227 countries and regions, Taiwan places last at 1.07 children per woman.



According to the CIA, TFR compares figures for the average number of children that would be born per woman if all women lived to the end of their childbearing years and bore children according to a given fertility rate at each age.

A TFR of about 2.1 children per woman is called replacement-level fertility, according to the UN Population Division. If the TFR goes under 2.1 children per woman, the population of a nation will start to shrink.

Taipei Fertility Center doctor Ruey-Sheng Wang said he fears that numbers of birth might be under 150,000 next year. 2022 is the year of Tiger which has been always the lowest due to Taiwanese beliefs.

The Taiwan National Development Council estimated that by 2070, there will be about 80,000 births and 328,000 deaths in Taiwan. The estimated population will be only about 15 million, which is 8 million less than the current 23 million.



Low birth rates plus death rates will impact the age structure of the society.

According to the Taiwan National Development Council, Taiwan has become an aging society in 1993 and an aged society in 2018. And by 2025, Taiwan will become a super-aged society.



The World Health Organization (WHO) and the United Nations define an “aging society” as one in which more than 7% of the population is 65 years or older, an “aged society” as a society in which more than 14% of the population is 65 years or older, and a “super-aged society” as a society in which more than 21% of the population is 65 years or older.

Over the years, there will be less working-age people, and the dependency ratio will be too high, which will become a burden for the working-age people.
