A BABY ISSUE?

LOW BIRTH RATES IN TAIWAN

PRESENT

By Jamie Lin Pinzon

Taiwan’s population continues to show negative growth in the first quarter of 2021.

According to the Ministry of the Interior, there were 34,917 births and 47,626 deaths in the first quarter of this year. The number of deaths was 12,709 more than those born.

In 2020, in the same period, there were 40,414 births and 46,967 deaths. Taiwan experienced negative population growth for the first time in 2020, with 165,249 births and 173,156 deaths.



President Tsai Ing-wen expressed her concerns of this issue.

“To me, declining birthrate is an issue of national security. If it is not resolved, it will not only be the financial burden of the country, but the pressure on the children will also be very heavy when they grow up.” she said in April, 2020.

People are living longer now, too. Life expectancy now in Taiwan is 80.95 years. At the same time, the age structure of Taiwan’s elderly population is rapidly aging.

In 2020, the proportion of elderly people over 65 will be 16.0%, and it will increase to 41.6% by 2070, which is about 4 out of every 10 people for the elderly over 65 years old.



In 2020, Taiwan’s dependency ratio was 40.1%, which means that every 100 working-age people currently needed to support 40 old and young people.

Professor Zhang Rongfu, Department of Social and Regional Development, from the National Taipei University of Education believes that Taiwan’s health insurance and annuity systems must rely on the continuous payment and taxation of the labor population to maintain their operations.

If the problem of declining birthrates is not improved, the labor population will decline and the elderly population will be reduced. The proportion will grow, and the aforementioned social welfare system will be under heavy pressure.
